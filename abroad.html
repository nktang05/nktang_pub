USC BISC 428 - The Biology of Health from a Global Perspective Oxford 2024

This 4 week summer program was a collaboration between USC and Oxford University exploring the social and environmental factors contributing to disparities in health, and efforts to achieve equity in health and health care throughout the world. We were introduced to the major diseases and risks to health in developing world countries, such as HIV, malaria, and COVID. We also had the opportunity to examine healthcare systems such as the UK system in a global context, consider strategies to use in resource-limited settings, and try to understand health policy in its wider social, economic, and political context.

USC GERO 499 - South Korea's Rapid Population Aging: How to Turn Challenges into Opportunities 2024

This 4 unit Maymester course explored multiple aspects of aging in the context of Korean policy, demographic trends, culture, interventions, and technology. We had the opportunity to visit Seoul and Andong, where we took part in activities and lectures to learn about gerontology and wellness in a rapidly aging country, visit multiple senior centers and learn about the services provided to the older population, wore an aging suit that mimics the physical challenges with aging, and learned about the importance of aging with dignity and staying engaged with your local community. While doing a paper-folding craft to support hand dexterity, I chatted with an older Korean gentleman about our favorite foods and daily life. That simple, genuine interaction taught me how shared interests can bridge cultural and generational gaps and helped me appreciate the beauty of everyday human connection.

USC GERO 486 - Aging and Older Adults in Latin America, Medellín Colombia, 2023

This 2 unit Julymester course provided a multifaceted perspective on the social, economic, health care, and environmental contexts experienced by older adults in Colombia, a Latin country with a rapidly aging population. We had the opportunity to visit Medellín Colombia, where we learned about the historical, economic and demographic changes in modern Colombia, the issues and progress affecting this dynamic city, including its innovative public transportation system, and opportunity to interact with locals at healthcare and senior care facilities, Universidad de Antioquia, and Comuna 13.

One of the special topics focused on the work of Dr. Francisco Lopera, a groundbreaking neurologist who undertook pioneering work in early onset Alzheimer’s disease and made immense contributions to understanding the genetic factors that drive Alzheimer’s disease through the discovery and long term study of a a large extended family in Colombia with a genetic mutation leading to a high prevalence of early-onset Alzheimer disease. His research has been instrumental in advancing our understanding of the disease and has provided valuable insights that may help prevent or delay its onset in future generations. Dr. Francisco Lopera was one in a million, and it was a privilege to have met him.
